History of Homeworlds

My main Homeworlds page

I might expand this someday into a narrative format, especially to incorporate a description of changes to the rules.

  • (1987) Andy Looney writes the short stories "Icehouse" and "The Children of Mars" describing a game played with small pyramid pieces.
    • Icehouse is described as a game with no turns and no board, but no such game exists yet.
    • Andy
  • (1989) John Cooper's rules for Icehouse are published. They largely match the description of the fictional game.
    • The same year, enthusiasts play game in the International Icehouse Tournaments.
    • Cooper writes for the fan newsletter "Hypothermia" under the name "Dr. Cool."
  • (1996) Hypothermia publishes rules for other games to play with Icehouse pieces.
  • (1998) John Cooper invents IceTraders.
    • John has said that early drafts of IceTraders were called Clusterf***.
  • (1999) IceTraders is distributed as part of "Icehouse: The Martian Chess Set."
  • (2000) Martian Chess Set wins Origins award for best abstract board game.
  • (2001) John Cooper modifies the rules of IceTraders, and the result is named Homeworlds.
  • (2002) The rules of Homeworlds are published for the first time in "Playing with Pyramids."
  • (2004) Looney Labs begins running two-player "Binary" Homeworlds tournaments at conventions.
    • Before 2004, Homeworlds was considered to be primarily a game for three or four players.
  • (2005) Aaron Dalton develops the website Super Duper Games (SDG) for playing board games by correspondence.
    • SDG includes the first online version of Homeworlds.
  • (2015) Draws by simultaneous elimination informally added to the rules.
  • (2015) Andy begins awarding physical medals to people who beat him at Homeworlds.
  • (2017) Babamots begin running The Great Homeworlds Tournament.
  • (2020) Homeworlds is distributed in a standalone box as a member of the Pyramid Quartet.
  • (2020) "thelast19digitsofpi" develops the website Homeworlds Live.
    • This is the first online version of Homeworlds designed for real-time play.
  • (2022) Board Game Arena officially releases a Homeworlds implementation that supports both correspondence and live play.
