Choosing your homeworld

Instafreeze

This is a trap you can set for new players when you move second.

If your opponent chooses a small star that is blue or green, you also choose a small star of that color as well as your first ship. You can build the last small piece of that color as a ship and freeze them out of that color (see below).

New players won't notice the problem and you can build mediums and larges of that color before they know what hit them.

Experienced players will respond by trading their initial (large) ship to enter the economy and the position will be about even.

Going second

If your opponent chose a small star, you probably shouldn't have a small star of the same color (unless you're attempting an Instafreeze).

They can get the last piece of that color as a ship and freeze you out.

Slow Doomsday Machines

Inevitable doom can take more than a day.

  • Slow doomsday (doomsweek?) machine
    Like a doomsday machine, but with a pair of medium yellows instead of larges. The first or last ship of each color can be moved singly with an ordinary yellow power move.
  • Extra slow (doomsmonth?) machine
    No yellow sacrifice at all, just move the six catastrophe ships one at a time. Importantly, the SECOND ship of each color should be large. Your opponent will normally attack the first ship after you move it in. Then you move in a large ship, and your opponent would like to reduce the concentration of that color by getting rid of the first ship (which they now own). But they must respond to the large by attacking it (assuming you have sacrificial red ships somewhere to make a large ship a threat). The third ship (of any size) can then move in for the catastrophe.

In the image below, south has assembled a doomsmonth machine.

Importantly, the doomsmonth machine does not usually work on red stars (see here).

No slow red catastrophe

Your opponent can't usually cause a red catastrophe by moving red ships one at a time. If they attempt this it,

  1. They move a red ship into your system.
  2. You attack the first red ship.
  3. They move another red ship into your system.
  4. You sacrifice the first red ship to attack the second red ship.
  5. They see the error of their ways.
For example, in the position below, South has just blundered by moving r3 into North's home. North should sacrifice the r1 and attack the invader. South has regrets.

You should check your own situation carefully, however. You must have a ship large enough to attack any invading reds, and—if you are defending a red system marker— you shouldn't let your only large ship be red. You won't be able to sacrifice the r3 to attack an invading r3, so a slow red catastrophe can happen when your opponent moves in with their second ship.
